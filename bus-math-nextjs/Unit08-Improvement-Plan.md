## Direction

Unit 08 should become a depreciation-centered unit.

The current "Year-1 Startup Model" scope is being refocused entirely around fixed-asset accounting, depreciation methods, and asset management. This gives the unit one clear instructional spine: teaching students to build, maintain, defend, and present a trustworthy fixed-asset register.

The enduring understanding for Unit 08 should be repeated early and often:

`Book Value = Cost - Accumulated Depreciation`

Everything in the unit should help students understand, calculate, test, defend, and present a defensible depreciation schedule and asset register.

- Depreciation provides meaningful accounting depth for a full unit after students have mastered inventory in Unit 7.
- The book value formula gives the unit one memorable anchor.
- Straight-line and double-declining balance methods create enough conceptual and Excel complexity on their own.
- The final workbook artifact becomes much clearer: prove that your asset register and depreciation schedules are reliable.
- Lessons 7-10 can mirror Units 6 and 7: guided rehearsal first, then independent transfer by group.
- Depreciation connects naturally to startup context: new businesses buy equipment, vehicles, computers, and must decide how to account for these long-term investments.

## Enduring understanding

The core formula for the unit is:

`Book Value = Cost - Accumulated Depreciation`

Students should leave the unit able to explain:

- what each part of the formula means
- why some costs are capitalized and others expensed
- how depreciation methods affect net income, taxes, and book value differently
- how a workbook can prove the depreciation schedule is trustworthy
- when straight-line versus accelerated depreciation makes business sense

## Core methods to teach in Unit 08

### Key Accounting Concepts

- **Capitalization vs. Expense**
  - when to record a cost as an asset versus an expense
  - the matching principle and long-term benefit test
  - materiality considerations and threshold policies
  
- **Depreciation Vocabulary**
  - **Cost**: the total amount paid to acquire and prepare the asset
  - **Useful Life**: how long the asset will provide value to the business
  - **Salvage Value**: the expected resale or scrap value at the end of useful life
  - **Book Value (Carrying Value)**: cost minus accumulated depreciation
  - **Accumulated Depreciation**: total depreciation recorded to date
  
### Depreciation Methods

- **Straight-Line (SLN)**
  - spreads cost evenly over useful life
  - simplest to calculate and understand
  - same depreciation expense each period
  - preferred when asset provides consistent value over time
  - often used for buildings, furniture, and assets with steady usage
  
- **Double-Declining Balance (DDB)**
  - accelerated depreciation method
  - front-loads depreciation expense in early years
  - twice the straight-line rate applied to declining book value
  - preferred when asset loses value quickly or provides more benefit early
  - often used for vehicles, computers, and technology equipment
  - must switch to straight-line in final years to avoid over-depreciating

### Method Comparison Considerations

- **Tax Implications**: different methods create different timing of deductions
- **Profit Presentation**: accelerated methods lower early profits but smooth later years
- **Cash Flow**: depreciation is non-cash, but affects reported profits
- **Investor Perception**: consistency in method choice builds credibility

## Lesson 4

**Status:** Needs complete rewrite

### Proposed title

`Double-Declining Balance: Accelerated Depreciation in Excel`

### Purpose

Introduce accelerated depreciation (DDB), teach the mechanics and when to use it, and begin simple spreadsheet implementation.

### Phase 1: Hook

- Compare a delivery van and a computer: which one loses value faster?
- Show how straight-line might not reflect reality for rapidly-depreciating assets.
- Introduce the concept of accelerated depreciation.

### Phase 2: Introduction

- Teach the double-declining balance formula: `Depreciation = (2 / Useful Life) × Book Value`.
- Explain why it's called "double-declining": twice the straight-line rate on declining book value.
- Teach the critical constraint: stop depreciating when book value reaches salvage.
- Explain when DDB is commonly preferred:
  - assets that lose value quickly
  - businesses wanting higher early deductions (tax benefit timing)
  - technology, vehicles, equipment with rapid obsolescence
- Compare DDB to straight-line: same total depreciation, different timing.

### Phase 3: Guided Practice

- Calculate DDB schedule by hand for Sarah's vehicle ($28,000, 5 years, $3,000 salvage).
- Show the decline pattern: Year 1 expense > Year 2 > Year 3, etc.
- Demonstrate when to switch from DDB to straight-line (conceptually, not advanced method).
- Teacher models simple Excel sheet for DDB calculation.

### Phase 4: Independent Practice

- Students build DDB schedule in Excel for Sarah's computer equipment ($15,000, 3 years, $1,500 salvage).
- Compare DDB results to straight-line results for the same asset.
- Write a brief recommendation: which method better fits this asset and why?

### Phase 5: Assessment

- Check DDB calculation accuracy (Excel or hand).
- Check conceptual understanding of accelerated depreciation timing.
- Include one item asking why DDB produces higher early expenses.

### Phase 6: Closing

- Summarize: DDB front-loads depreciation, matches rapid-value-loss assets.
- Preview Lesson 5 as side-by-side method comparison and workbook construction.

### Implementation sub-tasks
